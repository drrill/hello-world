# We need to talk about meetings

Meetings have a bad reputation, and most of it is earned. Ask your friends and colleagues what their favourite part of the job is, and "meetings" will never make the shortlist. Somehow we still can't quit them.

The usual response is to try to run them better: tighter agendas, clearer outcomes, assigned roles, a timekeeper. All fine. But before reaching for those tools, I like to start with Peter Drucker:

> There is nothing quite so useless as doing with great efficiency something that should not be done at all.

The question I've found most useful is not "how do we run this meeting better" but "does this meeting need to exist at all". The goal of any meeting should be one of three things: coordinate work, make a decision, or share information in a way that benefits from being live. Most of the meetings on your calendar fail that test. They could be an email, a Slack message, or a shared doc, and everyone involved would get their morning back.

That said, there are four meetings I keep on my calendar no matter what. They've earned their spot.

## Daily standups

Fifteen minutes, first thing in the morning, the whole team on the line. Three questions per person:

1. What did I get done yesterday?
2. What's on my plate today?
3. Do we need to rebalance or book time to dig into something?

The one hard rule: no content discussion. The second somebody starts explaining the design trade-offs of their current task, you're in a different meeting. Standups are about process, about flagging blockers, about making sure nobody is quietly stuck. When you catch a content topic, write it down and schedule it separately. The [Wikipedia entry on stand-up meetings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stand-up_meeting) has some good further reading if you want the history.

## Weekly 1:1s

Thirty minutes, every week, with every direct report. No exceptions. In my experience it matters less what you talk about than that you meet at all.

The agenda is set by the report, not by you. Talk about whatever they want to talk about most. Your job is to listen, to understand what's moving them this week, and to figure out where you can help.

New managers sometimes doubt 1:1s or offer to cancel them when things get busy. Don't take that bait. The 1:1 is your best tool for staying connected to your team, especially when everything else is on fire. Michael Lopp's [The Update, the Vent, and the Disaster](http://randsinrepose.com/archives/the-update-the-vent-and-the-disaster/) is more than a decade old and still the best write-up I've read on the topic.

## Monthly professional development chats

Looking back at my own career, the stretches when I grew the most were the stretches where somebody took a genuine interest in my development. That cuts both ways. The more I've invested in my team's growth, the better the team has gotten, and the more engaged people have been. Everybody wins.

Once a month, set aside real time for this. Give feedback on what's working and what isn't. Set a development goal together and revisit it. I usually bring a few notes based on what I've observed over the past four weeks and share them in advance so the person isn't reading cold.

If you do this well, at some point you'll be talking about your team members leaving, and that's a good thing. Mike Monteiro has a [great talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yBo0Y8OSGLE) that uses the seamstress analogy: the senior seamstress prepares the apprentice to go build her own business, client base and all. Growing people past the role you hired them into is the job.

## Weekly team meetings

This is the one I've gone back and forth on, but when it works, it earns its keep. Thirty to sixty minutes, first thing in the week. The standup is for today and tomorrow. The team meeting is for the whole month. A simple agenda I keep coming back to:

- What happened last week that we should all know about?
- How are we tracking against the metrics that matter?
- One or two topics we should actually debate as a group.

That's it. Four meetings. Everything else should be on a need-to-exist basis, and you owe it to your team to keep pruning. The best thing you can do for a calendar is delete something from it.
