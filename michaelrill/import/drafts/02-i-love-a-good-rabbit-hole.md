# I love a good rabbit hole

You know the kind. A thought crosses your mind, you look it up on Wikipedia or YouTube, and two hours later you're watching a documentary about some obscure pop genre and you don't feel even a little guilty about it. That's different from letting the algorithm spoon-feed you content, which always leaves me with the vague sense of having eaten a bag of chips for dinner. A rabbit hole is active. You're the one making the turns.

I fell into one the other night. I don't even remember how it started, but I was thinking about Sheryl Crow's [acoustic version of "Home"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CgQTepD7H3Y) and wanted to hear it again. YouTube was the only place I could find it, so I watched the [official video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j1Ij3rV_ZS4) as well, and the imagery caught me. I looked up the director, [Samuel Bayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Bayer), on Wikipedia, and before I knew it I was working through a small chunk of his catalogue:

- [Nirvana, Smells Like Teen Spirit](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hTWKbfoikeg)
- [Blind Melon, No Rain](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3qVPNONdF58) (yes, bumble bee girl)
- [The Cranberries, Zombie](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6Ejga4kJUts)
- [Hole, Doll Parts](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RD9xK9smth4)
- [Garbage, Only Happy When It Rains](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GpBFOJ3R0M4)

And that was the tip of the iceberg. One small observation: almost everything is shot in 4:3, which looks oddly alien in 2024. Bayer went on to direct videos for Bowie, Michael Jackson, Green Day and The Strokes, plus commercials for Nike and Coca-Cola. I turned off the TV around midnight feeling genuinely satisfied. That's not a feeling I ever have after a YouTube session that started with "hmm, what's trending".

Two things stayed with me the next day.

First, recommendation engines are optimised for the business model underneath them, which is advertising. They will happily serve you content that is engineered to be watched, but they're not serving you. That's why the candy feeling sets in. Tom Critchlow wrote a good piece called [Small b blogging](https://tomcritchlow.com/2018/03/22/small-b-blogging/) that pokes at the same nerve from a different angle: the web used to be a place you explored, and the platforms have quietly turned it into a place you scroll.

Second, consumption isn't the problem. Passive consumption is. The more actively I choose what to read, watch and listen to, the better the experience. It doesn't have to be useful in any productive sense. Interesting is enough. Gwern Branwen's short essay [On the hunt for web gems](https://gwern.net/note/early) makes the case better than I could.

My recommendation: next time you have a quiet hour, open Wikipedia, pick something you half-remember, and start following links. See where it takes you. The point isn't the destination. The point is being in the driver's seat again.
