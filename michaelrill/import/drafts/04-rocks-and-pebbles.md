# Rocks and pebbles

Writing is hard. Writing an executive summary is harder. You have maybe three minutes of someone's attention, no appetite for setup, and a pile of information that all feels important. The temptation is to try to say everything. The job is to say very little, very well.

The analogy that keeps me honest is rocks and pebbles.

Imagine you need to get your reader across a river without any of you drowning. There are four or five rocks big enough to jump on. Those are your key messages, the statements that, if a reader remembered nothing else, would still leave them with a working picture of what's going on. Around each rock is a handful of pebbles: the supporting facts, data points, or caveats that make it safe to land there. No pebble gets a sentence of its own in the summary. They live under the rock, ready if someone asks.

The failure mode of most executive summaries is that they try to lay a flat path of pebbles all the way across the river. Every stone gets the same weight. The reader runs out of patience halfway over and starts skimming, which is a polite word for drowning.

Finding your rocks is the editorial work, and it's the part that always takes longer than you think. Barbara Minto's [Pyramid Principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Pyramid_Principle) is the textbook on this if you want the full treatment, and Paul Graham's [Write Simply](https://paulgraham.com/simply.html) is the one-page version: if you can't say it plainly, you don't know it yet.

When a summary isn't working for me, I stop rewriting and ask a different question. What are the four rocks? If I can't name them, no amount of rearranging pebbles will fix the draft. And if I can, the draft usually writes itself.
