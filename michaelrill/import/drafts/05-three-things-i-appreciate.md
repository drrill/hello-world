# Three things I appreciate

The longer I do this, the more I notice what I'm drawn to in other people's work. None of it is glamorous. None of it shows up in performance reviews as a bullet point. But three things keep coming back to me, and when I work with someone who has all three, I go out of my way to work with them again.

**Finishing things.** There's a quiet skill in taking something from "almost done" to "done". Almost done is a wall most people give up at. They move on to the next exciting thing, leaving a trail of 90% projects behind them. I'm not against starting new things, but I've learned to trust people who finish. It's the difference between someone who can be relied on and someone who can't, and it's almost always visible from the outside.

Not everything needs to be finished at all costs. Priorities change, context shifts, sometimes a project deserves to be killed. That's fine. What I'm talking about is the default mode. When nothing has changed, do you still take the thing across the line?

**Committing fully.** If you're going to do something, do it properly. Half-hearted effort usually costs more than it saves, because you spend the whole time managing the gap between what you're actually doing and what you told yourself you were doing. Better to pick fewer things and go all in, or to say no upfront and mean it.

This isn't perfectionism. Perfectionism is a way of not finishing. What I mean is intention. Committing fully looks like being present in the room you said you'd be in, doing the work you said you'd do, at the quality you'd be happy to put your name on. Cal Newport's [Deep Work](https://www.calnewport.com/books/deep-work/) has the long version of this argument. The short version is: pick fewer things, then actually do them.

**Taking care of things.** This is the quietest of the three and the one I appreciate most. Maintenance doesn't photograph well. Nobody gets promoted for the documentation they updated, the dependency they bumped, the follow-up they sent so the ball wouldn't drop. But that work is the reason everything else keeps working. Stewart Brand has a lovely bit in [How Buildings Learn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_Buildings_Learn) about how the best buildings are the ones that invite small, continuous care, and I think the same is true of teams.

The people I'm talking about are the ones who patch the leak before the ceiling falls in. They update the runbook after the incident, not before the audit. They notice when a new hire looks lost and walk over. Nobody is going to write a case study about them. I'd hire all of them tomorrow.

Finishing. Committing. Taking care. Not a framework, not a process, not a set of values to laminate on a wall. Just three things I've learned to look for, and three things I try to be on the days I'm paying attention.
