# Be kind, and other rules worth remembering

I've always had a soft spot for the early 2000s show Scrubs. It's smart, it's funny, and every so often it hides a genuinely profound thought behind the laugh track. When the show moved to ABC for its eighth season, it suddenly shared a network with Sesame Street. The writers leaned into the coincidence and built an episode around it, complete with cameos from the Sesame Street characters. The episode closes with a short monologue from J.D. that has stayed with me ever since:

> And then I realized why I thought about Sesame Street all day. In a way, you can learn everything you have to know from watching it as a kid. Like, always play nice. Always try your hardest. And even, it's okay to cry.

That was sixteen years ago. I still think about it. The older I get, the more I notice how many of the lessons we learned as children are the same lessons we keep having to relearn as adults, just in more complicated packaging. Robert Fulghum's [All I Really Need to Know I Learned in Kindergarten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_I_Really_Need_to_Know_I_Learned_in_Kindergarten) made the same case in 1986 and it holds up. A few of the rules I find myself coming back to:

## Play well with others, and take turns

Taylor Swift said something in her recent docuseries that I keep thinking about. She was describing her approach to casting dancers: "I don't want dancers that blend in. I don't care about them pulling focus. I want them to pull focus. I want you to feel like you saw an entire crew of individual stars on the stage." I love that. It's a much better description of teamwork than most of what passes for it in corporate training.

A lot of workplaces still reward the quiet version of collaboration, the kind where nobody stands out and everyone colours inside the lines. That's not teamwork. That's just uniformity with a team name on it. The best teams I've been part of were the opposite: everyone had a clear point of view, everyone was willing to be visible, and everyone was also willing to step back and make room when it was somebody else's turn. Real collaboration is rotation, not suppression. You trust that the floor will come back to you.

## Always try your hardest

Ted Lasso has a line I love: some people don't want to keep score because then they'd have to say "that's the best I can do". The truth is that there's no shame in losing, or in failing outright, as long as you can say that honestly. What gets under my skin is when people hedge their effort on purpose so they have an out. It's a way of protecting the ego at the expense of ever finding out what you're actually capable of. Carol Dweck's [Mindset](https://www.mindsetworks.com/science/) is the long answer. The short answer is: try. All the way. You'll be surprised.

## Ask questions, and admit when you don't know

The fastest way to lose credibility is to fake it. The fastest way to build it is to say "I don't know, let me find out" and then actually come back with an answer. Nobody expects you to know everything. People do expect you to be honest about what you don't know. That's a lower bar than most people treat it as.

## Listen more than you speak

This one I still work on. The meetings where I learned the most were the ones where I talked the least. Not because I was being strategic about it, but because I was genuinely curious and the other person had something I needed to hear. Kate Murphy's [You're Not Listening](https://katemurphyauthor.com/) is a worthwhile read on this, if only for the chapter on how most of us treat listening as the thing we do while waiting to talk again.

## Clean up after yourself

Meetings, documents, code, inboxes, group chats, the kitchen. If you leave it better than you found it, people notice. They rarely say it. But they notice.

## Be kind

The one that has to come last because it's the one that frames all the others. Kindness isn't a bonus feature. It's not softness or niceness or avoiding hard conversations. The kindest thing you can often do is give someone clear feedback they can actually use. The kindest thing you can do in a meeting is not make it harder than it has to be. The kindest thing you can do as a manager is trust people with the full picture.

None of these are complicated. That's the point. They gain their power from how consistently we practice them, not from how clever they are. Every so often I try to check in with myself and ask whether I'm actually living them out in how I work and how I show up for the people around me. The answer is usually "mostly", which is an honest answer, and mostly is enough as long as you keep asking the question.

The things that make us good colleagues and good humans are, more often than not, the same things we absorbed before we could tie our own shoes. Sometimes the smartest move is to go back to the basics.
