# Pulse check

Once you hit a certain age, you get introduced to the annual physical. Thirty minutes with your doctor, a few blood tests, a reminder that you should probably drink more water. It's not a thrilling appointment, but it does the job. It catches the things you'd otherwise miss and gives you a rough sense of where you stand.

We do the same for cars. Annual service. Oil change. Tyres rotated. We do it for furnaces and chimneys and roofs. So it's only reasonable that companies do the same for their teams, and most of them do. It usually shows up as the annual engagement survey. Fifteen minutes to fill out, a few weeks of silence, a slide deck with the results, and then a full year before the next round.

For the company as a whole, that rhythm works. It catches the big structural stuff. For an individual team, though, I think it has three problems.

**Once a year is too slow.** Teams drift between surveys. A new manager, a reorg, a big launch, a quiet exit, any one of these can change the texture of a team in a week. If you're relying on an annual survey to catch that, you're already a few months too late. The early warning signs live in the hallway conversations, the quiet "do you have a minute" Slack DMs, the fact that the usually-chatty person has been silent in the last three standups. Those are your real instruments, and they need attention more than once a year.

**The questions are the wrong questions.** Company-level surveys ask company-level questions, because they have to compare across hundreds of teams. That makes the data clean and the action plans generic. The questions that would actually help your team are different from the questions that would help the team next door, and different again from the ones HR is tracking. If you never ask them, you never hear the answer.

**There's a lag between signal and action.** By the time you get the results back, half the things you'd want to respond to have either already resolved themselves or gotten worse. Either way, the moment has passed.

What I've found useful is a lighter, faster version of the same idea. A monthly check-in with the team, maybe ten minutes at the end of a regular meeting, where I ask two or three questions I actually care about. "What's felt heavy this month?" "Anything you'd change if you could change one thing?" "Who on the team should I be paying more attention to?" The questions change depending on what's going on. The frequency is the point. It lets you catch small things while they're still small. Patrick Lencioni's [The Advantage](https://www.tablegroup.com/books/the-advantage/) makes the same argument at book length.

The annual survey is fine. Keep it. Just don't mistake it for a pulse check. Your pulse doesn't take twelve months to respond to a change, and neither does your team.
