# Leadership economics: of fish and ponds

Here's a question I ask new founders more often than they expect: would you rather be a big fish in a small pond, or a small fish in a big pond? Said a different way, would you rather have one percent of a ten billion dollar market, or forty percent of a two hundred and fifty million dollar market?

The instinctive answer is that it doesn't matter, since in both cases you're a hundred million dollar business. That's the mathematically correct answer. It's also completely wrong.

Most startup pitches I've seen land on the big pond. The logic goes something like this: the market is huge, so even a small sliver of it makes us a real company. We fly under the radar of the incumbents because we're too small to notice. And because the pond is still growing, we have room to grow with it. It sounds like a hedge against gravity. What could go wrong?

Here's what goes wrong. If you're the small fish, someone else is the big one. That big fish has more capital, more distribution, more customers, and usually more patience than you do. They don't have to beat you on quality to beat you. They just have to outlast you. And every time they sneeze, you catch a cold. Hamilton Helmer calls this [counter-positioning](https://7powers.com/) in his book 7 Powers, and it's one of the most useful frames I've come across for thinking about competitive dynamics. The incumbent doesn't have to see you to crush you. They just have to move in your general direction.

The big fish in the small pond has a different set of problems and, in my experience, a much better time of it. You set the pricing. You know every customer by name. You define the category, which means you also define what it means to be good at it. When you move, the pond moves with you. The ceiling is lower, but the floor is much, much higher.

The thing nobody tells you about small ponds is that they grow. A focused company that dominates a small segment is the company best positioned to expand into adjacent segments, because it has the reference customers, the cash flow, and the brand to fund the move. Amazon didn't start as "the everything store". It started as the biggest online bookseller on the planet, and the bookseller paid for the everything. Ben Thompson's [Aggregation Theory](https://stratechery.com/2015/aggregation-theory/) has a good long read on how that dynamic plays out across categories.

So when I hear a founder tell me they're going after a trillion dollar market, I get nervous. When I hear a founder tell me they're going to be the default choice for a specific group of people in a specific context, I lean in. Small pond, big fish. Get good at the small thing first. The big thing will still be there when you're ready for it.
