# Using OneNote to replace your Moleskine

For the longest time I carried a physical notebook around. A proper one, with a good pen clipped to the cover. There's something about putting a pen on paper that helps me think. The tactile slowness of it forces me to choose my words, and the act of writing a sentence by hand feels more committed than typing one. I still use paper for specific things, mostly diagrams and raw brainstorming.

But I've also come around on OneNote. What finally won me over was realising that I was trying to solve two different problems with the same notebook, and paper was only good at one of them.

The first problem is thinking. Paper is great for that. The second problem is finding what I wrote down three weeks later, when I actually need it. Paper is terrible for that. I'd watched myself fill three notebooks in a row without ever once going back to read something I'd written, and then I'd catch myself trying to remember where I'd scribbled some half-formed idea. "I know I wrote this down" is a sentence I used to say a lot, right before giving up and writing it down again.

OneNote fixes the second problem without ruining the first, as long as you treat it with some discipline. Here's the system I've settled on:

1. **One note for each day.** Dated, always. That's the top-level container. Everything that happens on a given day goes into that day's note. Meetings, follow-ups, random ideas, the thing I want to remember to ask my manager next week. The date is the spine that lets me reconstruct what I was thinking about six months ago, which turns out to be surprisingly useful.

2. **Use headers to break up sections.** OneNote lets you collapse sections under headers, which matters more than I expected. A day's note can easily grow to twenty things. Without headers you end up scrolling through a wall of text. With headers you get a little table of contents that makes the note legible at a glance.

3. **Keep a separate notebook for print-outs and scribbles.** Screenshots, scanned receipts, the back of the envelope where I drew the diagram, all of that lives in a dedicated "reference" section so it doesn't clutter the daily notes. Paper things go in there via my phone camera. It's not elegant, but it works.

4. **Tag notes and sections within notes.** OneNote's tag feature is the thing I use the most. I have a small set of tags I reuse obsessively: "to do", "question for 1:1", "follow up next week", "idea for later". When I come back to a day's note, the tags tell me what still needs attention without me having to reread the whole thing.

5. **Live in search and recent notes.** Once the daily notes pile up, the navigation stops being about where things are and starts being about what they're about. I rarely open OneNote by clicking into a folder. I type a word I half-remember into the search bar, and that's how I find things. Recent notes is the back button for my brain on any given afternoon.

6. **Learn the keyboard shortcuts.** This sounds obvious and it's still the step most people skip. Ctrl+1 through Ctrl+6 for tags. Ctrl+Alt+1/2/3 for headers. Ctrl+. and Ctrl+/ for lists. The time I've saved since I stopped reaching for the mouse is probably measured in weeks by now. Myke Hurley and Federico Viticci's [Cortex podcast](https://www.relay.fm/cortex) has a whole episode thread where they argue about note-taking systems, and the one thing they agree on is that the system you can operate from the keyboard is the system you'll actually use.

The point of all of this isn't OneNote specifically. You could do the same thing in Obsidian, Apple Notes, Notion, Bear, or a plain text file on your desktop. Derek Sivers has a nice short piece on his [plain text everything](https://sive.rs/plaintext) workflow that makes the case for stripping the tool back even further. The point is that a notebook needs to be both a place to think and a place to remember, and most paper notebooks only do the first half.

I still carry a Moleskine. I use it for the bits of thinking that need to be slow and physical. Everything else lives in OneNote, which is searchable, backed up, and always with me. Somewhere in the middle is a workflow that actually works, and I'm not precious about which tool earns which job.
