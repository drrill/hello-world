# Having figured it out

I have a job at one of the biggest companies in one of the fastest growing sectors. My title sounds fancy when I say it out loud. The work I've done has, at various points, caused large amounts of money to change hands. By most reasonable external measures, I should feel like I've figured something out.

Do I? Not really. Not in any deep way.

That sounds like false modesty, and I don't mean it to. I mean it as a straightforward observation about how expertise actually feels from the inside. Every level of seniority I've reached has come with its own new version of "wait, I'm not sure what I'm doing here". The stakes get higher, the rooms get quieter, the problems get less well-defined. The thing that used to feel like the summit turns out to have been the base camp for the next climb. Penelope Trunk has a line I like: you're never going to feel ready, so stop waiting for the feeling. Adam Grant makes the same point in a different way when he talks about [confident humility](https://adamgrant.net/podcast/re-thinking-with-adam-grant-heres-how-im-rethinking-confidence/). Believe in your ability to figure things out. Stay honest about how little you currently know.

So no, I haven't figured it out. What I do have is a set of lessons I've picked up along the way, most of them the hard way, a few of them by accident. I thought it was worth writing some of them down, partly because doing so helps me notice what I actually believe, and partly because every time I've been willing to share the half-formed version of something, somebody has pushed back with a better version and I've walked away smarter.

Is this uncomfortable? Oh yes. Writing about leadership from a position of "I don't have this figured out" feels a little like giving a cooking lesson while apologising for burning the onions. But the alternative is waiting until I'm sure, and I'm not going to be sure. Neither is anyone else worth listening to. The honest voices in the room are the ones who'll tell you what they're still wrestling with, and those are the voices I've learned the most from. This post is me trying to be one of them, out loud, in public, where the blast radius of being wrong is manageable and the upside of being occasionally useful is worth it.

So consider this the opening note. Whatever I write after this, please read it with the understanding that it's a work in progress, and so am I. If that sounds like a cop-out, I get it. It's also the only honest way I know how to start.

Einfach machen.
