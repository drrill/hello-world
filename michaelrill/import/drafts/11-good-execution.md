# Good execution

A few years ago, I had the honour of taking the stage at [Ignite Sydney](https://ignitetalks.io/). Ignite is a presentation format with two unforgiving constraints: you get exactly five minutes, and your twenty slides auto-advance every fifteen seconds whether you're ready or not. It's the closest thing to public speaking in a batting cage. I can recommend it to anyone who wants to get better at editing themselves in real time.

My topic was what I'd learned about productivity by taking dance lessons with my wife. At the time I was newly married, and dance lessons took me out of my comfort zone on a weekly basis. Dancing turned out to be a surprisingly honest mirror for everything else I was trying to get better at, and in particular for the thing I want to write about here: the difference between planning and execution, and why most of us are quietly bad at both.

Productivity systems have been a guilty pleasure of mine ever since I first read David Allen's [Getting Things Done](https://gettingthingsdone.com/). Two ideas from that book have never left me. The first is that the brain is good at having ideas and terrible at holding onto them, which is why lists and inboxes exist. The second is that when you do the work to turn a vague intention into something "shovel-ready", you lower the activation energy for doing it. The job of a good planning system isn't to make you feel organised. It's to leave you with work that you can pick up without having to think about it.

That's the separation that matters: planning decides what to do, execution does it. The failure mode most of us fall into is trying to do both at the same time. We sit down to write a document and instead spend forty minutes deciding which document to write. We open a task list and spend the time renaming the tasks. The mode-switching is expensive, and it's invisible until you notice it.

## What good planning looks like

Planning isn't the same as making a plan. A plan is an artefact. Planning is the thinking you do to produce it, and the thinking is the part that matters. A few things I look for in a plan that will actually hold up when it hits reality:

**Transparent.** You can audit it. The assumptions are written down somewhere, even if only in a footnote. When the plan turns out to be wrong (and it will), you can point at the assumption that broke and update it. A plan you can't audit is a plan you can't correct.

**Realistic.** Plan for the worst and hope for the best. That doesn't mean padding everything with fear. It means naming the things that can go wrong, deciding which ones you'll plan for and which ones you'll accept the risk on, and writing that decision down so nobody has to relitigate it later.

**Honest about trade-offs.** Every plan is a choice to do one thing instead of another. If the plan doesn't say what you're not doing, it's not a plan, it's a wish list. The trade-offs are the plan. Will Larson's [Good Strategy, Bad Strategy](https://lethain.com/good-strategy-bad-strategy/) walks through this idea at length and is well worth a read.

**Easy to understand.** "Mom" is not a good label for a todo. "Call mom to ask about the hydrangeas" is. If your own shorthand confuses you two days later, imagine what it does to a team of ten trying to rally around it. Plans at the team level need to be simple enough to repeat from memory, in one sentence, without looking at the doc. If you can't do that, you don't have a plan yet.

## What good execution looks like

Once the plan is in place, execution is almost boring, and that's the point. I've come to think of good execution as three things.

**Focused.** You're doing one thing at a time, at the quality you'd want to see from someone else. No switching between tabs, no half-reading Slack on your phone while the other half of your brain tries to write the paragraph. The specific tool doesn't matter. The discipline does.

**Directional.** Execution without a direction is motion, and motion is not progress. This is where the plan earns its keep. Good execution asks "what's the next thing that would move me closer to the goal", not "what's the next thing on my list".

**Feedback-loopy.** Short loops between action and learning. Did what I just do get me closer or further from the outcome? If further, what do I change? Shape Up by Basecamp calls this [hill charts](https://basecamp.com/shapeup/3.4-chapter-13), and it's the best visualisation I've seen of the difference between "I've done a lot of things" and "I know where I am".

The dance-lesson version of all this was simpler. Before the music starts, decide which step you're practising. During the song, just dance it. After the song, ask the instructor what went wrong. Repeat.

Promise and deliver. Plan clearly, execute honestly, and keep the loop short enough that you can see yourself getting better. It's not a productivity system. It's just the shape of work that actually gets done.
