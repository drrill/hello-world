# On productivity

The Guardian has a wonderful habit of turning its long reads into audio, which is exactly what a long read should be. I was walking the dog when I listened to the [audio version](https://www.theguardian.com/news/audio/2017/jan/13/why-time-management-is-ruining-our-lives-podcast) of Oliver Burkeman's piece [Why time management is ruining our lives](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2016/dec/22/why-time-management-is-ruining-our-lives). Burkeman takes aim at the productivity industry, and since personal heroes of mine like Merlin Mann and David Allen make cameo appearances, I was both flattered and slightly on the defensive.

I should say upfront that I'm biased. I've read [Getting Things Done](https://gettingthingsdone.com/) more than once. I still find comfort in [Inbox Zero](http://www.43folders.com/izero). I keep a weekly review on my calendar. None of that has made me a productivity nihilist, which is the turn Burkeman's piece eventually makes. What it has done is clarify what I actually believe about productivity, and that turns out to be a shorter list than I expected.

His main point I completely agree with: optimising your life for efficiency will not, on its own, make you happy. In fact, taken to its extreme, it will quietly make you miserable. You will finish every day with the feeling that you could have done more, because more is always available. The system keeps producing work, and the better you get at it, the faster it produces. Oliver's follow-up book [Four Thousand Weeks](https://www.oliverburkeman.com/books) has the long version of this argument, and it's worth the read.

But I think he runs a couple of ideas together that should be kept separate, and the running-together is where my disagreement starts. Here are four things I want to be clearer about.

## Efficiency is only a small part of productivity

The piece uses "productivity" and "efficiency" as if they were the same word, and they're not. Efficiency is about the ratio of output to effort. Productivity is about whether the output was worth producing in the first place. A perfectly efficient day spent on the wrong thing is still a wasted day.

The reason I still care about productivity systems is that they help me figure out which thing to work on, not just how fast to work on it. That's David Allen's real contribution, which gets lost in the lifehacker-era caricature of GTD as a way to process email faster. The core move of GTD is "decide what it means". Once you know what something means, you can decide what to do about it. Most of the time you don't need to do anything. The act of deciding is the work.

## The perfect system fallacy

Every few years I talk to someone who has just discovered a new productivity system and is now rebuilding their whole setup around it. I recognise the look because I've had it. There's a particular kind of pleasure in tweaking the system instead of doing the work the system is supposed to enable. I'll own it: the hours I've spent comparing to-do apps are not hours I can get back.

Two things I've had to accept. First, there is no perfect system, so stop tweaking. Second, even if there were, the returns on getting closer to it diminish fast. The difference between a good system and a great system is small. The difference between no system and a good system is enormous. Most of the value is in showing up for the weekly review at all, not in whether the review template is optimal.

## There are no shortcuts

Every few years, a new app or a new book promises that you're one framework away from the productive life you want. I've bought most of them. None of them have been the thing. The actual work of becoming more productive is boring and slow. It looks like doing your weekly review when you don't feel like it. It looks like closing the tab with the thing that isn't your job. It looks like writing the first draft even though it will be bad. There's no hack for any of that.

Burkeman is right that the productivity industry sells a fantasy. Where I part ways is in thinking the fantasy invalidates the underlying practice. Meditation apps are not meditation. Weight-loss hacks are not exercise. And productivity books are not productivity. The real thing is still worth doing, even if the industry that wraps it up and sells it back to you deserves the skepticism.

## Purpose drives everything

The last point, and the one that makes the other three matter. If you don't know what you're trying to do with your life, no system will save you. You will be efficient at the wrong things and feel the subtle sickness of that for as long as you keep it up. The most useful productivity question I know is also the most uncomfortable one: if I keep doing what I'm doing, at the quality I'm doing it, for another year, will I be proud of what I've produced?

If yes, stop tweaking the system. Go do the work.

If no, the problem isn't that you need a better inbox. The problem is earlier. James Hollis writes about this beautifully in [Living an Examined Life](https://www.jameshollis.net/books), which is not a productivity book and is more useful than most of them. Figure out what you actually want your days to add up to. Then pick a system, any reasonable system, and use it to move toward that thing. The system isn't the point. The system is the scaffolding. The building is the point, and the building is yours to design.
