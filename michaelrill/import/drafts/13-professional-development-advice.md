# Professional development advice from Tom, Kylie, Drake and Kimmie

So you've made it past your first twelve months. You showed up, took notes, worked hard, had good ideas, and presented your work without visibly sweating. You're no longer the new person. You're one of the crew. And now you're sitting at your desk wondering: is that it? What's next? When do I get promoted?

The short answer is: not so fast. This is not German military service, where three months without shooting at your comrades gets you your first promotion. But there is a path, and I want to share what I've learned about walking it. Four steps, nine words, no hashtags:

1. Set goals.
2. Reflect.
3. Get feedback.
4. Love what you do.

Each one has a patron saint for the purposes of this post: Tom, Kylie, Drake, and Kimmie.

## 1. Set goals (Tom)

Tom is Tom Cruise, and more specifically his character Maverick in [Top Gun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_Gun). If you haven't seen it, stop reading and go watch it. I'll wait.

In the 1986 classic, young F-14 pilot Pete Mitchell is given the chance to train at the Navy's Fighter Weapons School at Miramar. He's barely made it into the academy. The first thing he does, standing around in aviator sunglasses and looking very sure of himself, is announce that he intends to win the trophy for the best pilot at the end of the course. It's brash and it's overconfident and it's also exactly the right move. Maverick set himself a goal. Spoiler: he doesn't quite get the trophy. That isn't the point. The point is that the goal gave him something to steer towards, which is the difference between flying with intent and just cruising along. (Sorry.)

I encourage everyone on my team to set themselves two kinds of goals: **contribution goals** and **personal development goals**. Contribution goals are the outcomes you want to produce. They should ladder up clearly to what your team is trying to achieve. If you can't draw that line, that's a conversation to have with your manager, today. A goal that doesn't connect to the bigger picture isn't really a goal. It's a chore.

Personal development goals are different. They're about who you want to become over the next year. The skill you want to get better at. The experience you want to have. The weakness you want to stop apologising for. Lofty and aspirational is fine for the goal itself. The experiences that get you there should be concrete and specific. Not "get better at public speaking", but "give one talk a month for the next six months, even if it's a five-minute update at team meeting".

Whatever you write down, write down the why as well. A sense of purpose is the best motivator I know, and it's the thing that will keep you going when the work gets hard. Don't wait for your manager to hand you a purpose. That's your job.

## 2. Reflect (Kylie)

Kylie is Kylie Minogue, specifically the version of her who wrote [I Should Be So Lucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_Should_Be_So_Lucky), which has nothing to do with reflection but everything to do with the fact that the song will be stuck in your head for the rest of this post. You're welcome.

Reflection is the part of professional development that almost everyone skips. It's unsexy, it feels indulgent, and it requires you to sit still. But without it, goals are just noise. You need time to look back at what happened and ask: what worked, what didn't, what did I learn, what would I do differently.

My rule is simple. Once a month, thirty minutes, pen and paper. No phone. I write down the highlights and lowlights of the month, one thing I learned, one thing I'd do differently, and one thing I want to carry forward. Derek Sivers has a [piece on daily journaling](https://sive.rs/journal) that makes the case even more strongly, and he's more disciplined about it than I am. Start with monthly, then go from there.

The goal of reflection isn't to be hard on yourself. It's to get into the habit of noticing. You can't learn from an experience you never looked at.

## 3. Get feedback (Drake)

Drake is the rapper, and also the patron saint of asking questions you're not sure you want the answer to. Getting feedback is uncomfortable in exactly the same way that stepping on a scale after the holidays is uncomfortable. The solution is the same: do it anyway.

Share your goals. I know that sounds like the kind of thing a corporate poster would tell you. I also know how much resistance most people have to saying out loud what they're trying to get better at, because saying it out loud makes it real, and making it real makes you accountable. That's the feature, not the bug.

What's the worst that can happen? Someone gives you bad advice. The best? Someone gives you advice that changes the trajectory of your next year. I've had that happen more than once, and every single time, it started with me telling somebody what I was working on and then actually listening to what they said.

Google publishes its [OKR process](https://rework.withgoogle.com/guides/set-goals-with-okrs/steps/introduction/) in part so that everybody internally can see what everybody else is working on. It's a big deal and it's the right call. Shared goals make collaboration possible. Hidden goals make it accidental.

## 4. Love what you do (Kimmie)

Kimmie is [Kimmy Schmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt), from Tina Fey's show, who after being rescued from a doomsday cult walks into Manhattan with an unreasonable amount of enthusiasm for everything. That's not naivety. That's a choice. It's the choice to find something to love in the work in front of you, even when the work is hard.

You don't have to love every part of your job. Nobody does. But you do have to find something in it that you're genuinely glad to be doing, because the parts you love are the ones that will carry you through the parts you don't. Steve Jobs' [Stanford commencement address](https://news.stanford.edu/2005/06/14/jobs-061505/) gets quoted for "stay hungry, stay foolish", but the better line in the speech is earlier: "the only way to do great work is to love what you do". Stated as an ultimatum it sounds impossible. Stated as an instruction, it's the thing I try to do every morning. Find the part I love. Start there.

## A small exercise

Close this tab. Open whatever you use for writing.

Write down one development goal you want to work on between now and the end of the financial year. Make it specific. Make it honest.

Then write down two or three things you're actually going to do to get there. Concrete actions. On your calendar. With dates.

That's it. That's the whole thing. Tom, Kylie, Drake and Kimmie will be here when you get back.
