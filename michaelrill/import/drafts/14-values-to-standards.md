# Values to standards

Every company has some version of values. Most of them are a collection of inspirational attributes arranged on a wall. The classic in this genre is Enron's, which were famously [Communication, Respect, Integrity, Excellence](http://www.nytimes.com/2002/01/19/opinion/enron-s-vision-and-values-thing.html). They sound great. They kept sounding great right up until the company folded, which is when everyone noticed that nobody had actually been practising them.

Values on a wall are the corporate version of inspirational quotes in your Facebook feed. You read them, you feel warm and fuzzy for a second, and then you go about your day. Nothing has changed, and nothing will, because values that stop at the wall are not values. They're marketing.

The version I keep coming back to: **values are meaningless until they become standards.**

A value says what you care about. A standard says what you do about it. A value is "we care about quality". A standard is "we don't ship code without a review from someone other than the author". A value is "we respect each other". A standard is "meetings start on time, and if you're the senior person in the room, you don't talk first". The standard is the part that has teeth. The standard is the part that's visible from the outside. Ben Horowitz makes a version of this argument in [What You Do Is Who You Are](https://a16z.com/book/what-you-do-is-who-you-are/), and the title alone is worth the shelf space.

Here's a way to check whether your values have become standards. Pick any one of them. Can you name the last time someone at your company was held to it? Can you name the last time someone got a hard "no" because of it? If the answer is "we talk about it in all-hands", that's not a standard, that's a slogan. If the answer is "we didn't hire this person, we delayed this launch, we let this customer go", now we're talking.

In the end, the values that matter are the ones that get told as stories. "Remember when we pulled the feature the week of launch because the team wasn't comfortable with the privacy implications?" That's a story. Stories travel. Stories get retold at onboarding. Stories are how a value becomes a standard, and how a standard becomes a culture. Ed Catmull's [Creativity, Inc.](https://www.pixar.com/creativity-inc) is full of these stories from inside Pixar, and it's the best argument I know that culture is built out of specific decisions, not general aspirations.

A few years ago I did an exercise with a team I was leading that I'm still proud of. We took the official company values, which were fine as far as wall art goes, and we asked a single question of each: what does this value actually ask us to do on Tuesday afternoon, in the specific work we are doing this week? We wrote the answers down. Some of them were obvious. Some of them were uncomfortable. One of them was "stop saying yes to things we know we won't deliver". That last one took about twelve months to actually stick. But twelve months later, it had become something the team expected of itself, and new people absorbed it from day one.

That's the move. Take the value off the wall and ask what it means on Tuesday. If you can't answer, it isn't a value yet. And if you can, write the answer down and hold each other to it.

Culture is velocity. And velocity is made of standards.
