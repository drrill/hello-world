# Software people

Ask most people what software is and they'll describe an app, a website, or a full-blown product suite. For a long time that definition was fine, because software was easy to identify by the device it ran on. Before the 1970s, software mostly meant numerical problems loaded onto mainframes via punch cards: flight trajectories, payroll, actuarial tables. In the 1970s, keyboards made it possible to work with text, and software widened to include word processing and accounting. By the 1990s, computer shop flyers were advertising "multimedia" as if it were a separate product category, which is how you can tell that it wasn't a product category yet. The machines just started accepting more kinds of input at once.

Today the definition has collapsed. A modern smartphone has so many sensors and is so well-connected to its environment that the old question of "what does this software do" has become almost meaningless. Software does whatever it's pointed at. Your fridge, your car, your doorbell, your running shoes. The thing we used to call software is now everywhere, and most of it is invisible. Marc Andreessen called this [software eating the world](https://a16z.com/why-software-is-eating-the-world/) back in 2011. He was right, and the meal is still going.

Which is why I want to write about something else. I don't think software is the interesting story anymore. I think **software people** are.

By software people I don't mean software engineers, or not only them. I mean a way of approaching problems that is increasingly recognisable regardless of what the person's job title says. Software people treat the world as something you can iterate on. They break problems down, ship a small version of the solution, watch what happens, and adjust. They assume that a system can be changed, that any workflow can be automated, and that a good feedback loop is worth more than a perfect plan. They're as likely to show up in a finance team or a city hall as they are in an engineering org. Marc Benioff's [Philanthropy Cloud](https://www.salesforce.org/philanthropy-cloud/) is one example of software people showing up somewhere software people didn't used to show up. There are thousands of others.

The interesting shift, in my experience, is that the hard part of building software is no longer the code. Code is cheap, and getting cheaper. What's scarce is the judgement about which code to write, the instinct for what to ship first, the taste to know what "done" should look like, and the willingness to keep the thing alive after launch. None of those are technical skills in the old sense. All of them are cultural ones. Paul Graham's [The Hundred-Year Language](http://www.paulgraham.com/hundred.html) is a long-range version of this argument, and even the parts that have aged have aged in interesting ways.

Here's what I've come to believe. A modern team doesn't need more software. It needs more people who think like software people. People who believe the status quo is negotiable. People who see a weekly manual report and ask why it's still manual. People who can tolerate a version one that's genuinely rough because they know version two is coming on Friday. People who read a postmortem and look for the systemic fix, not the scapegoat. People who can explain what they did in plain language to someone who doesn't care about the stack.

If you're managing a team, this is the quietest and most important shift to watch for. It doesn't show up on an org chart. It shows up in the stories people tell about what they're working on, and in the things they stop tolerating. It shows up in who gets invited to fix the next broken process, and in the way meetings start to end with "let's try it on Monday and see" instead of "let's set up another meeting to align".

Software isn't a feature. It's a lifestyle. And increasingly, the people who have adopted that lifestyle are the ones making the biggest difference inside organisations, whether or not they've ever opened a terminal.

That's the thing to hire for. That's the thing to develop in the people you already have. The code will keep getting easier. The people will still be the hard part.
