# Operating models

There's a line from Patty McCord, former head of HR at Netflix, that I keep pinned in my notebook:

> You know why playing a game is fun? Because it has rules, and you have a way to win. Picture a bunch of people showing up at an athletic field with random equipment and no rules. Someone is going to get hurt. You don't know how to play, you don't know how to score, and you don't know how to win.

My current job description, roughly, is to go make sure a large software organisation knows how to play its own game. The technical term for this is "developing an operating model". The practical meaning is: figure out how the work actually moves through the org, who decides what, how things get prioritised, how good looks, and how we know we're winning. Write it down. Get people to agree to it. Notice when it breaks. Fix it. Repeat.

It sounds boring, and in the worst version of it, it is. Operating models have a reputation for generating slide decks that nobody reads and boxes and arrows that nobody can decode. I've produced some of those myself, and I'm not proud of it. When an operating model is bad, it's usually because it was built in a conference room by people describing how they wish the org worked, rather than by people describing how the org actually works and then changing the specific things that need to change.

The best operating model I've ever helped build was not a slide deck. It was five answers to five questions, printed on one page, that the team went back to every week.

1. **What game are we playing?** Not "our mission", which is usually too abstract to be useful. Something concrete. "We are the team that makes sure every Office user can get value from Copilot without writing a prompt." That kind of thing. The thing you could say to a stranger in a lift.

2. **How do we know we're winning?** The handful of metrics that, if they move, everything else is likely moving with them. Not the dashboard with forty charts. The three numbers the team would want to see if they only got to see three.

3. **Who decides what?** Which decisions live with the team? Which live with the lead? Which escalate, and to whom? If two people can both claim to own a decision, you have a gap. If nobody can, you have a bigger gap. The Netflix culture deck still has the [best treatment](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture) I've seen of the "context, not control" idea that underlies this question.

4. **How does work get in, and how does it get out?** The intake process for new work. The definition of done. The handoffs to other teams. This is the most operational of the five questions and also the one that surfaces the most hidden friction the first time you try to answer it honestly.

5. **How do we get better?** The feedback loop. Retros, reviews, postmortems, whatever you call them at your company. The point is that the operating model is a living thing. If you're not regularly noticing where it's failing and updating it, you don't have an operating model. You have a memorial to the one you had a year ago.

Marty Cagan's [Inspired](https://www.svpg.com/books/) walks through a version of these questions specifically for product teams, and [Team Topologies](https://teamtopologies.com/) is the closest thing I've seen to a general theory of how to shape organisations that can answer these questions at scale. Both are worth the read.

The trap to avoid is treating the operating model as the output. The operating model is the tool. The output is a team that knows how to play its own game well enough to keep winning, and when the game changes, knows how to redesign the rules fast enough to still have fun. That's the part I care about, and that's the part worth spending time on. Everything else is boxes and arrows.
