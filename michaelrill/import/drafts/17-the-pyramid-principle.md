# The pyramid principle

Here's a small taxonomy of things I've noticed in the places I've worked. Bad writing is a meeting machine. A book that should have been a blog post. A blog post that should have been a tweet. A tweet that should not have been published. A meeting that should have been an email. An email that should have been a text message. A text message that should have been "yes" or "no". And on it goes.

The cost of each step up in the wrong direction is enormous, because every unclear sentence adds up to somebody's time. Somebody who has to read it, re-read it, decide what it's actually asking, and then schedule a meeting to find out. The cheapest thing in the world is a clear thought. The most expensive thing in the world is an unclear one being slowly unpacked by a room full of people.

[Barbara Minto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_Minto) figured out how to fix this in the 1970s, while she was working at McKinsey. Her method has a dry name and a life-changing effect. It's called the Pyramid Principle, and if you write for work, it's the most useful thing you will ever learn that isn't a shortcut.

The Pyramid Principle is built on a simple idea. Put the answer first. Then the reasons that support the answer. Then the evidence that supports the reasons. Answer, reasons, evidence. Top of the pyramid down.

That ordering is the opposite of how most of us naturally write, which is why the method feels awkward at first. When you've just done the work, you want to take the reader through the journey you took. You want to walk them through the problem, the options you considered, the things you ruled out, and then triumphantly land the conclusion at the end. That's a perfectly good story. It's a terrible memo.

The executive reading your memo is not taking a journey. She has ninety seconds and seven other memos in the queue. She needs the answer. If the answer is compelling, she will read on for the reasons. If the reasons are compelling, she will check the evidence. If at any point she stops reading, the part she read first was the part that did the work. The pyramid builds for that reader, not for the author.

There's a small writing exercise I do whenever I'm stuck with a draft. I stop trying to fix the sentences and instead ask: what's the one sentence I'd want the reader to remember? If I can write that sentence, I put it at the top and the rest of the document suddenly has a spine. If I can't write it, the problem isn't the draft. The problem is that I haven't yet decided what I think. No amount of rewording will fix that. Only thinking will.

Paul Graham's [Write Simply](https://paulgraham.com/simply.html) and George Orwell's [Politics and the English Language](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/) are good companion reads. Both arrive at the same destination as Minto from a different angle: writing is thinking, and unclear writing is almost always a sign of unclear thinking. The fastest way to write better is to think better, and the fastest way to notice that your thinking is unclear is to try to write the top of the pyramid first.

So here's my one-line challenge. Next time you're working on anything longer than a paragraph, before you write a single word of the draft, write down the sentence you want your reader to remember. Look at it for a minute. Ask whether it's actually true, and whether you believe it, and whether a smart reader would care. If the answer is yes, build the rest of the document around it. If the answer is no, go back to thinking. The writing isn't ready yet, and no amount of polish on the sentences will hide it.

Top of the pyramid first. Everything else is support.
