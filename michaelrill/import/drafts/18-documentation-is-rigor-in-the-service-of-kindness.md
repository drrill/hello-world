# Documentation is rigor in the service of kindness

It's hard to get anyone excited about documentation. Writing things down is considered a chore by most people, right up there with filing expenses and updating the project tracker. It lives on the same list as the things you do because you have to, and the moment you have permission to skip it, you do. I've been guilty of this more times than I'd like to count.

Then a few years ago I heard a phrase that reframed it for me permanently. I can't remember who said it first, and if you know, please tell me. The phrase is: **documentation is rigor in the service of kindness.**

Let's take those two halves one at a time, because both are doing work.

## The rigor part

Writing something down is an act of thinking more carefully than speaking requires. When I say something out loud in a meeting, I can be vague and still get away with it. Tone, context, a shared glance across the table, all of those carry the sentence across the gap between my brain and the listener's. The listener fills in the bits I didn't quite say. That's fine for conversation. It's a disaster for lasting decisions.

When I write a decision down, I lose the tone and the glance and the shared context. I'm left with just the words. And the words, to actually mean something, have to be more precise than I had to be in the meeting. I have to pin down what I actually think. Which option are we picking, and why? What are the specific trade-offs? What assumptions are we making that might stop being true? Who is going to do what by when? I've had many meetings where I walked out feeling like we'd agreed on something, sat down to write it up, and realised halfway through the first paragraph that we had agreed to three different things depending on who you asked. Writing catches that. Speaking doesn't.

This is why the writing-first cultures at places like [Amazon](https://www.justinmares.com/how-amazons-6-pager-meeting-format-keeps-them-productive/) and [Basecamp](https://basecamp.com/handbook/how-we-work) produce so much better decisions than the average meeting-driven org. The six-page memo isn't an aesthetic choice. It's a rigor device. The act of writing it forces you to think clearly enough that your thinking survives contact with other humans. Jeff Bezos talks about this in his [2017 letter to shareholders](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/2017-letter-to-shareholders), and once you read it, you start to see the shape of it everywhere: the best thinkers are usually the best writers, and it's not a coincidence.

## The kindness part

This is the half that converted me. Because I used to think of documentation as a tax on the present, something you pay now so that a future you or a future teammate has an easier time. That's true, but it undersells it.

Documentation is the form of care you give to the people who will come after you on a piece of work. It's the way you say to the new engineer on the team, "I know you weren't in the room when we made this decision, and I want you to understand it the way I understand it, because you deserve to." It's the way you say to the person picking up the on-call pager at 2am, "I've been where you are, and here's what I wish someone had written down for me." Documentation is a way of pre-empting the frustration of someone you'll never meet.

That framing changed how I write documents. I stopped writing them for my manager, who was never going to read them carefully anyway. I started writing them for the next person on the team, the one who doesn't exist yet, who is going to try to figure out what on earth we were thinking when we picked this architecture. I write for her. I tell her what we considered, why we made the call, what we were worried about, and what we'd do differently if we had to make the decision again. It's a letter to a stranger I care about.

Mike Bland has a [wonderful essay](https://mike-bland.com/2022/01/31/rainbows-of-death.html) on what he calls "rainbows of death", which is about the internal cost of letting knowledge live only in people's heads. Dan Luu has written [repeatedly](https://danluu.com/) about the same phenomenon from a different angle. Both of them land in the same place: the health of a team over time is measured by how much of what it knows is written down in a place where someone who isn't in the room can find it.

## Rigor plus kindness

Put the two halves together and you get something useful. Documentation is rigor because it forces you to think more clearly than you otherwise would. Documentation is kindness because the main beneficiary is a future human, usually not you. When I catch myself resenting the task of writing something down, I try to remember both halves. I'm not doing it for the project tracker. I'm doing it because I owe the next person on this work the same clarity I wish I'd been handed.

Most of the documentation I've written in my career has never been read. I'm fine with that. Most fire extinguishers never get used either, and nobody argues that we should stop installing them. The ones that matter are the ones that are there when you need them, and you don't get to choose in advance which ones those will be.

Write it down. Not for yourself. For the next person. Even if that person turns out to be you, six months from now, wondering what you were thinking. They will thank you, quietly, in a way you'll never hear. That's the whole point.
