# Removing friction

After twelve years away from it, the 2024/25 season was the one we finally got back into skiing as a family. We bought boots that fit, we found a nearby resort that wasn't a full day trip, and we committed to a pass instead of paying by the day. None of that was cheap, and most of it felt, at the time, like overkill for a hobby we weren't sure we'd stick with.

We stuck with it. The difference between the last time I skied and this season wasn't my skills. (My skills are exactly where I left them, which is to say, modest.) The difference was friction. Every previous attempt to get back into skiing had required a small mountain of decisions the night before: do we have the gear, do we have the tickets, when do we need to leave, where are we parking, where are we eating, where are the kids going to rent their stuff. Any one of those was solvable. All of them together were enough to make Saturday at home sound like the better option, and Saturday at home usually won.

This year we removed almost all of them in advance. Boots, skis, poles, helmets, already in the car. Season pass, already paid for. Usual route, usual lot, usual place for lunch, on the calendar as a recurring event. By the time Saturday morning came around, there was almost nothing to decide. We just went. And once we'd been twice, going a third time was trivial, because the habit had a groove to run in.

I think about this a lot, because I see the same pattern everywhere, and almost all of it is in the friction. BJ Fogg's [Tiny Habits](https://tinyhabits.com/) has a line I love: "motivation is unreliable, ability is adjustable." If the behaviour you want is harder than the behaviour you'd default to, motivation has to carry the gap, and motivation runs out. If the behaviour you want is easier than the default, or at least not much harder, motivation barely has to show up. Ability is adjustable. So adjust it.

Here are a few places I've started applying this, in work and outside it.

**Writing.** I used to open a blank page and stare at it. Now I keep a running file of half-formed drafts, bullet points, links, and stolen sentences. When I want to write, I don't start from zero. I start from the nearest half-finished thing and see where it wants to go. The friction went from "write a post" to "find a draft and push it a bit". The first one feels impossible on a Tuesday night. The second one feels like picking up a book you've been reading. Austin Kleon's [Steal Like an Artist](https://austinkleon.com/steal/) is a good companion read on this, specifically the bits about keeping a "swipe file".

**Exercise.** Putting the running shoes next to the bed the night before is the entire hack. There is no deeper secret. Anything that makes it one decision instead of three will survive a bad morning.

**Meetings.** The agenda in the calendar invite, not in a separate doc. The link to the last meeting's notes, right at the top. The action items in bold at the bottom. Every time I've seen a team struggle with "running better meetings", the fix has turned out to be friction-removal rather than any new ceremony. Make it trivial to prepare. Make it trivial to remember what was decided. Make it trivial for the next person in the room to catch up. The meeting gets better by itself.

**Getting back into a hobby.** Buy the boots. Pay for the pass. Put it on the calendar. The thing you're trying to do will still be hard in all the interesting ways, and you will earn every turn you take. But the pre-work won't be the thing that stops you. Whatever you buy on day one will pay for itself in the Saturdays you actually show up for.

The default attitude when something isn't happening is to assume the problem is motivation. "I just need to want it more." That's almost always wrong. The problem is almost always friction, and friction is solvable. You don't have to become a new person to get the thing to happen. You have to put the new person's life within reach of the current person's energy level, and then let the current person show up a few times. The new person will catch up.

We're going skiing again this weekend, and I've already stopped thinking about it. That's the whole trick.
