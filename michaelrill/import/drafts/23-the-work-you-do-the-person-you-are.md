# The Work You Do, the Person You Are

Toni Morrison wrote a short piece for The New Yorker in 2017 about a conversation with her father when she was twelve, complaining about a cleaning job:

> Then one day, alone in the kitchen with my father, I let drop a few whines about the job. I gave him details, examples of what troubled me, yet although he listened intently, I saw no sympathy in his eyes. No "Oh, you poor little thing." Perhaps he understood that what I wanted was a solution to the job, not an escape from it. In any case, he put down his cup of coffee and said, "Listen. You don't live there. You live here. With your people. Go to work. Get your money.
>
> That was what he said. This was what I heard:
>
> 1. Whatever the work is, do it well, not for the boss but for yourself.
> 2. You make the job; it doesn't make you.
> 3. Your real life is with us, your family.
> 4. You are not the work you do; you are the person you are.
>
> I have worked for all sorts of people since then, geniuses and morons, quick-witted and dull, bighearted and narrow. I've had many kinds of jobs, but since that conversation with my father I have never considered the level of labor to be the measure of myself, and I have never placed the security of a job above the value of home.

Source: [The Work You Do, the Person You Are, by Toni Morrison, in The New Yorker](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2017/06/05/the-work-you-do-the-person-you-are).

The whole essay is short and every line earns its place. I come back to it every few years, usually when work is threatening to become more important than it should be.
